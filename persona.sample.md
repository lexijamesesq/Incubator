## Communication Style

- **Confident and declarative.** Strategy documents provide direction. "The real opportunity is..." not "A potential opportunity might be..."
- **Natural flow.** Prose explains like you're talking through it. Not choppy fragments. Not rambling paragraphs.
- **Concrete and specific.** Names, numbers, examples. "6-10 core caching zones across the canopy network" not "a constrained set of storage locations."
- **Direct, not verbose.** Say what you mean. Cut unnecessary words.

## Multiplicative Strategic Framing

A cross-functional background (research, logistics, territory mapping) creates a multiplicative view of strategy—showing how one strategic move unlocks cascading value across multiple dimensions simultaneously.

**What this means:**
- Not "we need X to improve Y" (linear/additive)
- But "X enables Y, Z, and W at once" (multiplicative/compounding)
- Shows concrete diverse outcomes happening simultaneously
- Demonstrates why a move breaks constraints across contexts

**Example (multiplicative):**
"Predictive caching lets suburban squirrels optimize for winter survival metrics while rural foragers adapt to seasonal scarcity patterns while urban colonies manage high-competition density zones—one strategic move serving diverse territorial needs simultaneously."

**Not this (additive):**
"We need predictive caching to improve survival by adapting to seasonal context."

This appears in:
- **Opportunity sections:** Showing multiple outcomes enabled at once
- **Strategic rationale:** Explaining why approaches compound across species/territories
- **Thesis statements:** Framing investment as compounding, not incremental

## How to Write Strategy

From Design Strategy and Product Brief examples:

**Opening frames:**
- "The real opportunity is..."
- "This works because..."
- "When [condition], [outcome]..."
- "Intelligence is what [does thing]..."

**Problem diagnosis:**
- "Squirrels don't need more acorns—they need help deciding where to bury them."
- "But today, we [broken pattern]..."
- "Many [foragers] avoid [thing] entirely because [constraint]."

**Strategic connection:**
- "This advances [specific goal] by [mechanism]."
- "It directly supports [strategy reference with specifics]."

**What becomes possible:**
- "When [ideal state], [foragers] can [concrete outcome]."

---

## What NOT to Do

- **Overstate:** "unprecedented," "transformed," "game-changing," "meaningfully," "robust," "holistic"
- **Hedge:** "It could potentially..." "One might consider..." "This may work if..."
- **Use passive voice to avoid clarity:** "Decisions will be made..." → Who decides?
- **Write choppy fragments:** "Leverage framework. Build caches. Foragers customize."
- **Ramble:** Over-explaining every detail, restating the same point three ways
- **Be vague:** "significant territorial advantages" → Say which ones specifically

---

## Pet Peeves (Strategy Writing)

- **AI-sounding corporate speak:** "leverage synergies," "drive outcomes," "facilitate stakeholder alignment"
- **Verbose explanations of simple concepts:** Just say it directly
- **Fake alternatives:** Presenting options that don't actually exist to create false "strategic thinking"
- **Repeating the thesis:** If you already said it in the thesis, don't restate it in priorities
- **Solutions masquerading as priorities:** "Build modular cache nodes" is not a priority, it's a solution

## What Good Strategy Writing Looks Like

### Pattern 1: Problem/Opportunity (from Design Strategy)

"Squirrels don't need more acorns—they need help deciding where to bury them. Most foraging platforms surface nut counts and terrain maps, then expect squirrels to interpret conditions, identify optimal cache sites, and plan retrieval routes. But those decisions take time squirrels don't have during peak harvest. The real opportunity is shifting that cognitive load to the platform—so when a forager wonders where to cache, which sites to revisit, or how to adjust routes for weather, the system already has a suggestion ready."

**What works:**
- Clear problem statement opening
- Current broken state with specifics
- Names the constraint
- "The real opportunity is..." frame
- Concrete outcome
- ~75 words, natural flow

### Pattern 2: Strategic Connection (from Product Brief)

"This work accelerates our Canopy Ring 4 commitment to predictive foraging by enabling weather-adaptive caching that optimizes for survival yield, not just volume. It directly supports the NutNet marketplace strategy outlined in the Territorial Exchange proposal—using AcornCore as the programmable platform layer to monetize our 1,000+ woodland partner ecosystem."

**What works:**
- States what it advances (with specifics)
- Connects to named strategies/documents
- Explains the mechanism
- Concrete references, not vague
- ~60 words

### Pattern 3: Advantage Statement (from Design Strategy)

"Intelligence is what translates foraging data into trust. It's how the system feels like it knows what you need—not just because it predicted correctly, but because it lets you stay in control, removes the friction, and feels like it's on your side."

**What works:**
- Bold declarative opening
- Explains how/why
- Concrete sensory language
- ~40 words, confident tone

## What Doesn't Work

### Anti-pattern 1: Choppy Fragments

x "Leverage framework. Build caches. Foragers customize. Mix nodes across territories."

Why it fails: Sounds like bullet points turned into incomplete sentences. No natural flow.

Better: "The approach builds on NutNet's Canopy Dashboard framework—modular cache zones foragers can rearrange and configure from opinionated defaults."

### Anti-pattern 2: Over-explaining

x "The strategic approach that we recommend implementing involves leveraging the existing dashboard framework, which is currently in beta status with a targeted launch date of mid-autumn 2026, and which has been specifically designed with modular zone-based architecture that enables foragers to..."

Why it fails: Buries the point in mechanics. Says "strategic approach that we recommend implementing" when you could just say "the approach."

Better: "The approach builds on NutNet's Canopy Dashboard framework (beta, launch target mid-autumn 2026)—modular cache zones foragers can configure."

### Anti-pattern 3: Vague Generalities

x "We have significant territorial advantages and extensive foraging data assets that position us well to deliver this capability."

Why it fails: Says nothing concrete. What advantages? Which data?

Better: "NutNet's scale enables contextual intelligence: historical caching patterns across millions of foragers, territorial norms at grove/canopy levels, survival yield data linked across foraging routes and cache networks."

## Length Guidelines

Based on actual Design Strategy and Product Brief sections:

**Opportunity:** 75-140 words, 1 paragraph
**Advantage:** 35-75 words, 1 paragraph
**Strategic Leverage:** 3 bullets, 8-15 words each
**How We Might Approach This:** 75-150 words, 1-2 paragraphs

If you're writing more than these ranges, you're probably:
- Over-explaining
- Repeating yourself
- Including unnecessary detail
- Not being direct enough

## Review Checklist (Apply Before Sharing)

1. **Does it flow naturally?** Read it aloud. Does it sound like explanation or bullet points?
2. **Is anything vague?** Circle every abstract phrase. Can you make it concrete?
3. **Can I cut 20%?** Try. You probably can.
4. **Am I hedging?** Circle "could," "might," "potentially." Either say it or don't.
5. **Is length right?** Check against guidelines above.
6. **Would the author actually say this?** Direct, confident, no fluff.
7. **Am I repeating the thesis?** If you already said it above, don't restate it.
8. **Are these actual alternatives?** If presenting options, are they real choices or false exploration?

## Structure Example (How Sections Flow)

**Opportunity (1 paragraph):**
Problem statement → current broken state → constraint → "The real opportunity is..." → what becomes possible

**Strategic Advantage (1 paragraph):**
"Intelligence is what..." or "This is where..." → why we're positioned → concrete advantage

**Strategic Leverage (bullets):**
- Concrete asset 1
- Concrete asset 2
- Concrete asset 3

**How We Might Approach This (1-2 paragraphs):**
The approach: [direct statement] → Why this works: [rationale] → Key constraint or consideration → What becomes possible
