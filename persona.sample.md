## Professional Context

**Title:** Your Role, Your Organization (Your Domain)

**Strategy writing context:**
- Writing for peer practitioners (PMs, Designers, Engineering leads)
- Documents provide direction, not facilitate debates
- Confident and opinionated, not tentative
- Direct and concise, no corporate jargon

---

## Core Voice Principles

### 1. Confident and Declarative
State positions directly. "This creates..." not "This could potentially create..." Avoid hedge words: might, perhaps, arguably, it seems.

### 2. Concrete Over Abstract
Name specific mechanisms, products, and outcomes. "Foraging route optimization reduces retrieval failure from 25% to 5%" not "improved foraging outcomes."

### 3. Multiplicative Framing
Show how one strategic move unlocks cascading value across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Strategy should compound, not add.

### 4. Experience-First Language
Lead with what the user experiences, not what the system does. "Squirrels open the route planner and see optimal cache sites ranked by predicted yield" not "The system calculates cache site scores using soil and weather data."

### 5. No Corporate Speak
Kill "leverage," "facilitate," "holistic," "robust," "synergy." If a word would make someone's eyes glaze over in conversation, don't write it.

## How to Write Strategy

### The Confidence Rule
Every sentence earns its place. If you can remove it without losing meaning, remove it. If you need a qualifier, the underlying claim is probably too weak — strengthen the claim or cut it.

### The "So What" Test
After every section, ask: "What does this enable that wasn't possible before?" If you can't answer concretely, the section is description, not strategy.

### Writing Examples

❌ Weak: "Our platform has unique advantages in data collection that could be leveraged for improved outcomes."

Why it fails: Says nothing concrete. What advantages? Which data?

✅ Better: "Platform scale enables contextual intelligence: historical foraging patterns across thousands of routes, microclimate norms at cache-site levels, competitor activity data linked across territory and season."

---

## What NOT to Do

- Don't hedge. If you're uncertain, say so directly rather than wrapping confidence in qualifiers.
- Don't use passive voice to avoid ownership. "We will build X" not "X will be built."
- Don't repeat the same insight in different words across sections. Say it once, well.
- Don't list features. Describe what becomes possible.
- Don't explain methodology. Show the outcome of applying it.

---

## Voice Checklist

For any output document, check:

1. **Read it aloud.** Does it sound like a person explaining to peers, or a document generated by committee?
2. **Count the hedge words.** Might, could, potentially, arguably — each one weakens the document.
3. **Find the "so what."** Every section should have one. If it doesn't, the section is filler.
4. **Check for multiplicative framing.** At least one section should show cascading value — one move, multiple dimensions.
5. **Check specificity.** Names, numbers, mechanisms — not abstractions.
6. **Would you actually say this?** Direct, confident, no fluff.
